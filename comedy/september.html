Oliver Twist's phone number

One day, a colleague asked, “Marissa, do you have Professor Charles Twist’s phone number?”
I said, “No, but you can call his brother.”
She blinked. “Who’s his brother?”
I grinned. “Oliver Twist, of course.”
She frowned. “But I don’t have Oliver Twist’s phone number either.”
At that point, I sighed. The joke was lost on her.
“Of course you don’t,” I said. “Oliver always wants some more anyway. Just call HR."

Angry bluebird

I'd been watching this little bluebird dad in my backyard, and my heart went out to him. He was working so hard, flying back and forth all day to feed his family. So when I found a big, juicy grub while gardening, I thought I’d help the guy out. I popped it right into the birdhouse.

Big mistake. HUGE.

Apparently, in the bluebird world, leaving a fat grub in another man’s house while he's out is the ultimate sign of disrespect. He didn't see a free meal; he saw a home-wrecker hitting on his wife.

The next day, that bird went full-on Rambo. He sat on top of that birdhouse like a tiny, furious king, launching kamikaze attacks on anything with feathers. My peaceful backyard turned into an avian war zone, all because I tried to be a good neighbor. Lesson learned: Don't leave unsolicited grubs on someone's doorstep. You might just start a gang war.

My Car's Drinking Problem

The first time and last time I ever got drunk, I was so convinced I was sober that I was ready to take my car to a mechanic.

After a German Embassy reception, I was driving friends home. Now, I’d never been drunk before, so I honestly felt fine. The car, however, did not. It kept veering wildly onto the sidewalks.

My friends, white-knuckled in their seats, yelled, "Marissa!"

Annoyed at my car's betrayal, I told them, "I know, I know! It's acting crazy! I have to take it in to get fixed first thing tomorrow."

My friends went dead silent for the rest of the ride.

I was the one who drank — but it was the car that got drunk.

I can read minds

After a late-night session of watching the Columbo detective show with my dad, I announced to my sister the next morning that my meditation had paid off - I could read minds.

"I'll prove it," I said. "Pick a number between one and five." "Three," she said, rolling her eyes. "Okay," I replied calmly. "Now open the dictionary that's been on the couch next to you the whole time. Turn to page 300." Tucked inside was a slip of paper that read: "I know you would choose three."

The look on her face was priceless. My brothers ran in to see what the pandemonium was about, and they were all stumped. There were no other strips of paper in the dictionary. They begged me to do it again. "Okay," my sister said, "Two!" I told her to look under the desk calendar on the TV. Sure enough, another note: "I know you would choose two." They were losing their minds.

My secret? I had notes for one, two, three, four, and five hidden all over the room. That's what they get for not watching Columbo with us.

Where is the switch?

I was with my team from Glaxo - medical doctors, waiting right outside - and I popped into the bathroom. I saw a woman using a hand dryer and I was like, "Cool!"

I finished up, went over to the dryer... and I couldn't find the switch! I looked from above, below, at the sides, everywhere! The darn switch was nowhere to be seen. Then, someone else walked in. Total panic! I pretended like I was just admiring the wall, trying to look casual. I didn't want to look like an ignorant idiot!

As soon as she went into a stall, I was back at it, frantically searching. I finally admitted defeat, wiped my wet hands on my jeans, and walked out. My team asked what took so long. My brilliant reply after my epic battle with technology? "Oh, nothing."

I don't speak English

A nurse told my husband he had to wait in the car because of COVID restrictions. I looked at her with wide, innocent eyes and asked, "But who is going to translate for me?"
Her: "You... you don't speak English?"
Me: "Not a word."
Her: "So you can't understand anything I'm saying right now?"
Me: "Precisely."
The genuine distress that washed over her face was a sight to behold. It took her a full ten seconds to process that I was perfectly explaining my inability to speak English... in English.

Monet's garden in Giverny

I learned a valuable lesson at Monet's garden in Giverny: if you're going to play dumb, you have to fully commit.

After getting bumped one too many times while trying to take a video in a dense crowd, I hopped a "Do Not Cross" rope onto a pristine, empty patch of lawn. It was perfect... for about 30 seconds, until a staff member marched over.

Her: "Hey, you can't be on the lawn. Didn't you see the sign?"

Me (thinking I'm a genius): "Me no speak English."

Her (not impressed): "Bullshit. You just understood my English."

Without even thinking, my mouth blurted out, "I did?"

Yeah. My brilliant plan lasted all of five seconds.

Having a cow

Apparently, "having a cow" does not involve any actual livestock. I learned this the hard way.

One day, my step-daughter announced, "My mom is having a cow." My brain immediately conjured up this horrifying image of her mom literally... biting a live cow. I suggested, "That's a horrible image. Can't you just say she's 'eating beef'?" After a world-class eye-roll, she just sighed, "Forget it," and walked out of the room.

Learning American slang is a minefield. Imagine someone says, “Break a leg” and you literally act on it.

Six countries in three days?

An immigration officer in Brussels nearly got a heart attack. He was going through the standard questions, looking at my passport.
Officer: "How many countries are you visiting on this trip?"
Me: "Six countries."
He nodded, getting his stamp ready.
Officer: "And how many days will you be staying?"
Me: "Three days."
He froze. The stamp hovered mid-air. He looked up at me, his eyes wide with a mix of awe and deep suspicion.
Officer: "Six... countries? In three days?"
It took a second to realize he meant the entire trip, while I was only answering for our three-day stop in Belgium. Whoops. For a moment there, I'm pretty sure he thought he'd caught the world's most overly ambitious, and possibly insane, tourist.

The cheapest mistake in Annecy

We were in a foul mood after being stung with a €25 toll on the way to Annecy, France. So when we immediately found ourselves on another toll road by mistake on our way to dinner, we made a cranky-tourist decision: we saw the open gate and drove straight through without a ticket.

Spoiler alert: you can't get off without a ticket.

Stuck at the next gate, my husband launched into a rapid-fire English explanation that basically spun the poor guard's head. He just held up a hand and said, "Attendez, je vais appeler le responsable." (Wait, I'm going to call the supervisor.)

Oh, great. We pictured it all: a one-hour wait, a lecture in French, and a fine big enough to ruin our appetite. After a long call - likely describing the crazy Americans he'd trapped - the guard turned to us with a deadpan look and held out his hand. "Fifty cents." The relief was incredible. We paid the man and got out of there like we'd just pulled off the world's cheapest, dumbest heist.

Marissa's rice soup

My cooking skills once received the most brutally honest two-part review in history.

Part One: After I spent three hours making rice soup for the sick Mother Superior, she took a single sip and declared, "This is terrible. I won't eat it." Ouch. I was convinced she was wrong.

Part Two (The Tie-Breaker): I summoned a stray dog as the ultimate impartial judge. I poured the soup into a bowl. He sniffed it, looked up at me with what I can only describe as great pity, and trotted off.

That’s when it hit me. "Oh," I thought to myself. "It's that bad. Huh."
